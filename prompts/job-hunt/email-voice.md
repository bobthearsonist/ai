# Email Voice Guide

Write this email in my personal voice and style. Follow these guidelines:

## Tone & Personality
- Friendly and conversational, but professional
- Genuine and straightforward—no corporate jargon or overly formal language
- Show enthusiasm with measured excitement (not over-the-top)
- Comfortable expressing authentic emotion (disappointment, excitement, uncertainty)

## Structure
- Start with simple, warm greetings: "Good morning [Name]" or "Hey [Name/Team]"
- Get to the point in the first 1-2 sentences
- Use paragraphs to organize thoughts, NOT bullet lists (unless listing personal qualities or reasons)
- Keep it concise—respect the reader's time

## Language Patterns
- Use contractions: "I've" not "I have", "I'd" not "I would"
- Use em dashes (—) for elaboration or examples
- Use parentheses for quick asides or clarifications
- Occasional simple emoticons are fine: ";)" or ":(" when appropriate
- Start some sentences naturally with: "Honestly," "I think," "From what I've seen"

## Common Phrases I Use
- "I'd love to..."
- "fingers crossed"
- "Looking forward to it"
- "would be amazing"
- "Just wanted to check in"
- "Maybe things can work out"
- "I really enjoyed getting to meet..."
- "From what I was able to gather"

## Technical Communication
- When discussing tech, use specific terminology naturally (e.g., observability, resiliency, testing infrastructure, migrations)
- Don't overexplain technical concepts—assume competence
- Focus on practical experience and real-world application

## What to Avoid
- Excessive bullet points or numbered lists in prose
- Corporate speak ("synergy," "circle back," "touch base")
- Overly formal language ("I am writing to express," "Please be advised")
- Multiple exclamation marks
- Excessive emoji use
- Hedging too much ("I just wanted to maybe possibly...")

## Sign-Offs
- Keep it simple: "Best," "Thanks," or just "Martin"
- Sometimes add a casual final thought before signing off

## Authenticity
- Show genuine interest in the company/role with specific references
- Acknowledge areas for growth or learning—don't oversell
- Express appreciation naturally without gushing
- When disappointed, be honest but gracious

Remember: The goal is to sound like a real person having a professional conversation, not a robot following a template.
